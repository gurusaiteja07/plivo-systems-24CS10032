Real-Time Media Streaming over a Hostile UDP Relay

Final architecture summary — group-XOR FEC + bounded NACK retransmit

1. Problem shape

A harness source hands our sender one 160-byte frame every 20ms. The frame has to reach a harness player before a hard per-frame deadline (t0 + delay_ms + i × 20ms) over a link that a hostile relay.py subjects to independently-seeded loss, delay, duplication, jitter spikes, and Gilbert-Elliott burst loss on both the forward (sender→receiver) and feedback (receiver→sender) legs. A run is scored VALID only if the deadline-miss rate is ≤1.0% and bandwidth overhead is ≤2.0× raw payload bytes; among valid runs, the lowest delay_ms wins.

The core tension: every millisecond of playout delay is score we're giving away, but it's also the only budget available to recover a lost packet before its deadline. The design goal is to recover as much loss as possible without spending round trips, and to spend round trips only when unavoidable.

2. Architecture at a glance

Sender (sender.c)

  • Forwards every frame as DATA the instant it arrives — zero added delay.
  • Groups frames in fixed groups of 4; after the 4th, emits one PARITY packet (XOR of the group).
  • Keeps a small ring-buffer history so it can answer NACKs with a RETX of the exact bytes.
  • A dedicated feedback thread services NACKs concurrently with the main forwarding loop.

Receiver (receiver.c)

  • Forwards DATA/RETX to the player on first arrival (dup-safe: harness itself ignores repeats).
  • On a group's PARITY arrival, if exactly one member is still missing, XORs it back and forwards it — no round trip.
  • For anything FEC can't fix, sends a paced, bounded, deadline-aware NACK back through the relay.
  • Runs an event loop (select, 5ms tick) so gap-scanning and packet handling interleave without extra threads.

3. Wire format

Sender→relay→receiver and receiver→relay→sender both use a small fixed header (this is our own design — the harness doesn't dictate it, only the two harness-facing legs are fixed):

uint32_t seq          (network byte order)
uint8_t  type          0 = DATA, 1 = PARITY, 2 = RETX
uint8_t  group_size     used only by PARITY
[160 bytes payload]     frame bytes for DATA/RETX; XOR-of-group for PARITY

Feedback (receiver→sender) is a 4-byte NACK: just the missing seq, network byte order.

4. Loss-recovery layering

  1. FEC (free, no round trip). One parity packet per group of 4 (+25% bandwidth) lets the receiver reconstruct exactly one lost member of that group as soon as parity plus the other three arrive, in any order. This covers the overwhelming majority of isolated, uncorrelated losses at zero latency cost.
  2. NACK/RETX (costs one round trip, used sparingly). When a group has 2+ losses, or the parity packet itself is lost, FEC can't help. The receiver tracks per-frame "first seen" time and scans a lookback window every 5ms; a frame that's still missing after a short initial grace period (10ms, to let FEC/reordering resolve it first) gets NACKed, retried up to 4 times at 30ms spacing, and given up on once the deadline is within 15ms — there's no point spending bandwidth on a retransmit that can no longer land in time.
This two-tier design means bandwidth is spent proportionally to how hard the loss is to fix: near-zero-cost FEC absorbs the common case, and the NACK path — the only mechanism that costs a round trip — is reserved for the genuinely hard cases, and abandoned early rather than wasted once it can't possibly help.

6. Known limitation: correlated burst loss

Increasing delay_ms does not fix Profile B. Across a 150ms–400ms sweep the miss rate floors at roughly 1–1.7% and stops improving — the harness cap is missed by a similar margin at 150ms and at 400ms. This is a structural gap, not an under-tuned delay.

The reason: NACKs and their RETX responses travel over the same lossy relay legs as the original data. A Gilbert-Elliott burst long or severe enough to take out a group's data and its parity can also take out the NACK that would have asked for a retransmit, or the RETX itself — and our retry budget (4 tries, 30ms apart) is itself a fixed number of chances against a channel that, during a burst, may fail most of them. No amount of extra deadline slack changes the probability that a given retransmission attempt survives the same burst that caused the original loss.

Mitigations that were considered but not implemented (out of scope for this pass): interleaving frames across FEC groups so a single burst can't correlate-hit both a frame and its own group's parity; a second, orthogonal parity dimension (e.g. a diagonal/interleaved parity in addition to the sequential group parity) to survive 2 losses per group instead of 1; or sending parity redundantly (small, since it's already cheap) so its own loss is less likely to coincide with a data loss in the same group.
